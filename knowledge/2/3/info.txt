Summary of the Paper:
The paper examines the role of investor psychology and rational reasoning in explaining the subprime financial crisis. It focuses on three main biases: overconfidence, loss aversion, and mimetic behavior. The authors propose a new conceptual model of financial decision-making to represent stock market dynamics during the crisis period. They construct an artificial financial market with two types of investors: institutional (rational) and individual (irrational), including noise traders and mimetic investors. Through simulation, the paper concludes that the interaction between rational and irrational behavior, as well as investor psychology, plays a crucial role in explaining financial crises. Overconfidence, loss aversion, and mimetic behavior amplify disturbances in the market and limit arbitrage.
